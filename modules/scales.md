# Scales and appearance

In [Psychometrics](psy.md) we examined psychometric functions, which describe how an
observer's choices (detection, identification, discrimination) change as a function
of an externally manipulated stimulus property. We also saw several ways responses
can deviate from the standard psychometric function:

- **Task-related factors**: lapses of attention and non-stable criteria can make it
  look like there is more noise than there really is.
- **'Appearance' factors**: the magnitude or quality of the psychological experience
  doesn't vary linearly with the scale of the externally presented stimulus property.

So far we have focused on measuring *sensitivity* (how much noise). But what if we
don't just want to account for appearance factors — what if we actually want to
measure how things *appear*? What is a psychological experience 'like'?
(Nagel, 1980.)

One way to measure 'what it is like' is to measure the relative similarity to
something else — and one way to measure relative similarity is to measure how
different something is.
