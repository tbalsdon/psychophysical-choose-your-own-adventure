# Adaptation

:::{note} Where you are
Part of [Methods of manipulation](man.md).
:::

Sensory adaptation was traditionally hailed as the 'psychophysicist's microelectrode'
(Frisby, 1979) because it enables inferences about the underlying properties of
neurons coding particular aspects of perception. The principle is simple: relatively
prolonged exposure to a particular stimulus changes the neural response to it, and so
changes our perception. This occurs from very low to very high levels of processing.
Stare at a bright light and you see a dark spot afterwards (retina). Watch downward
motion (a waterfall) and a static texture then appears to drift upward — likely a
relatively low level of visual cortex, area V5/MT (Kohn & Movshon, 2003). The effect
was popularised as the 'waterfall illusion' by Addams in 1834:

> "Having steadfastly looked for a few seconds at a particular part of the cascade …
> and then suddenly directed my eyes to the left to observe the vertical face of the
> sombre age-worn rocks immediately contiguous to the waterfall, I saw the rocky face
> as if in motion upwards, and with an apparent velocity equal to that of the
> descending water."

A simple way to understand adaptation is **response fatigue**: neurons responding to
a stimulus property become tired, reducing their responsiveness. The mechanisms can
be far more complex (Solomon & Kohn, 2014) — adaptation has even been proposed as
optimal resource allocation (Gepshtein, Lesmes, & Albright, 2013). High-level
examples are striking: adaptation to gaze direction occurs across faces of different
identities and sizes, with only the gaze direction held constant (Clifford & Palmer,
2018).
