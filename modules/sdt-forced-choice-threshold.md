# A test against Threshold Theory

:::{note} Where you are
*Forced-choice designs* ▸ part 2 of 2 (up to the
[forced-choice](sdt-forced-choice.md) overview).
:::

Let's pretend that Threshold Theory were true. Key point 3 is that absent is a
discrete state. In this case, the 2AFC task itself doesn't make much sense: either
the signal surpasses the threshold and the observer responds as they would in a
yes/no task, or the signal does not surpass the threshold and the observer has to
guess between two equally absent experiences.

What happens when we compare performance across these two tasks under Threshold
Theory?

Threshold Theory cannot predict how much performance increases in the 2AFC task,
because the 'discrete absent state' suggests a certain proportion of trials must be
guesses. Measured performance across 2AFC vs Yes/No tasks can also be used to
discredit Threshold Theory.

:::{important} Take-away 4
Observers have a continuous experience, where one signal-absent trial is
experienced differently from another signal-absent trial.
:::
